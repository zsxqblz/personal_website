Superconductor-Semiconductor Intergration in Nitride Materials Family

Background:

Superconductivity allows for current flow without resistance at low temperatures, making it attractive for energy-efficient computing and power transmissions. What's more, this mysterious phenomenon originates entirely from the quantum-mechanical interactions between the electrons and the crystal. The quantum nature allows for quantum computers made with superconductors.

This project investigates the integration of nitride superconductors (NbN, TiN) with nitride semiconductors (GaN, AlN). With a crystal growing technique called molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), we are able to lay superconductors and semiconductors on top of each other with "atomically-clean" interfaces: atomic bondings are exactly matched at the interfaces. MBE has enabled us to integrate semiconductor transistors on top of NbN superconductor and observe interesting effects.

NbN/AlN/NbN Josephson Junctions:

Currently, we are working on a device called the Josephson junctions. It consists of two layers of superconductor (NbN) sandwiching a very thin layer (~1 nm, around 8 atoms) of insulator (AlN). Because AlN is very thin, quantum tunneling can happen between the two NbN layers, creating many interesting effects. Josephson junctions play important role in metrology, precision sensing, energy-efficient computers, as well as quantum computers.


Technical Details - Josephson Junctions Fabrication Process

The fabrication of the NbN/AlN/NbN Josephson junctions is a top-down process as follows.

  • Growth of the NbN/AlN/NbN stack
  • Mesa device isolation
  • Etch the top layer to define the device geometry
  • Deposit SiO2 for electric isolation
  • Etch SiO2 to expose the top / bottom NbN
  • Contact formation

Currently, we are experiencing significant contact resistance and potential leakage in the oxide, and we are still working on it.
