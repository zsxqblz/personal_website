Tight-Binding Model for Oxide Semiconductors

Background:

Ga2O3 and Al2O3 are often referred to as ultra-wide bandgap semiconductors. The definitive property of a semiconductor is that there is an interval of energy that electrons are not allowed to stay at, and this interval is called the bandgap. The bandgap is closely related to many key properties of the semiconductor and its performance. In particular, a semiconductor with a wider bandgap can tolerate higher electric fields without burning down. This allows the device to operate at higher voltages. Alternatively, the device can be made smaller with similar performance, but now it can operate much faster. Also, a wide-bandgap device can operate at higher temperatures.

Si, the most common semiconductor on the Earth, has a bandgap of ~1 eV (eV means electron volt and is a very small unit of energy). On the other band, Ga2O3 has a bandgap of ~5 eV, and its breakdown field (highest field before burning) is ~26x higher than Si. Al2O3 goes even higher. This makes Ga2O3 and Al2O3 particularly attractive for high-power, high-frequency, and high-temperature applications. Ga2O3 and Al2O3 also enable semiconductor photonic devices in the UV spectrum, which narrow bandgap semiconductors are unable to achieve.

Why Tight-Binding:

Successful applications of Ga2O3 and Al2O3 require accurate modeling of them to aid in the understanding of the physics and the designing of the devices. Ga2O3 and Al2O3 have complex electronic structures (shown on the right) so they cannot be described by simple models, for example, the effective mass theory. On the other hand, Modern first-principle techniques such as density functional theory (DFT) are computationally demanding, so they cannot be applied to large-scale device simulations.

Tight-binding model offers both. It is computationally cheap: tight-binding only requires eigendecomposing a ~20x20 matrix, while for DFT the size is ~5000x5000. Meanwhile, it offers the full electronic structures of the materials. Additionally, a tight-binding model is defined on the level of orbital interaction at each atomic site, so it can be applied to study very precise structures like the interface between two materials (shown in the figure on the left).


Technical Details - Construction of the Tight-Binding Model

We derive tight-binding models using a Wannier function basis. Wannier functions are a convenient basis for tight-binding models because they are derived from the underlying band structure of the material, are formally orthogonal, can be localized to atomic sites, and preserve the site symmetry and coordination.

The procedure for deriving the tight-binding model is the following:

  • Calculate the DFT bandstructure (Fig. (a)).
  • Form the Wannier functions through selective localization. The exemplary Wannier functions are plotted in Fig. (b).
  • Write down the DFT Hamiltonian on the Wannier basis. Extract the energy of the Wannier functions and nearest-neighbor and next-nearest-neighbor couplings. These are the matrix elements of the tight-binding model and they accurately describe the lowest 4 conduction bands. (Fig. (c)).
